Budget snapshot for Buenos Aires

Prices in Argentina can change quickly with inflation, but many international students describe Buenos Aires as relatively affordable for food and going out, with housing being the biggest fixed cost. A rough ballpark is around ARS 600,000 per month including shared rent, food, transit and going out.

Example monthly budget:

  • Rent (shared flat / homestay): ~ARS 330,000
  • Groceries & café time: ~ARS 120,000
  • Subte & buses: ~ARS 30,000
  • Going out, concerts & fútbol: ~ARS 90,000
  • Misc / buffer: ~ARS 30,000

Why study in Buenos Aires?

Buenos Aires feels like a mix of European architecture and Latin American energy: wide avenues, old cafés, bookstores, political murals and late-night dinners where people are still ordering dessert at 1am.

As a student, you’ll juggle language immersion, long café study sessions and weekend ferias or fútbol games. This guide is here to give you a realistic sense of budgets, neighborhoods and rhythms beyond the tango stereotype.

Neighborhoods to know

Programs place students across the city, but these barrios come up a lot for international students.

Palermo

Parks · Bars · Cafés · $$$

Huge, trendy and full of bars, restaurants, boutiques and tree-lined streets. Great if you like going out and being where “everything happens,” but it’s one of the pricier pockets of the city.

San Telmo

Cobblestones · Antiques · Tango · $$

One of the oldest areas, with cobblestone streets, old buildings and a big Sunday market. Quieter during the week, busier on weekends. Beautiful if you like history and a slightly bohemian feel.

Recoleta

Elegant · Museums · Plazas · $$$

Leafy and elegant with classic architecture, museums and calm plazas. Feels more upscale and residential, good if you like quieter nights and studying in cafés or parks.

Quick tips for students

  • Dinner at 10pm is totally normal. Pre-game your hunger with a late merienda (coffee + pastry) around 6–7pm.
  • The Subte + buses cover a lot of ground; get a SUBE card early and always keep a little balance loaded.
  • Portuñol is real, living here is a great chance to practice Spanish listening with lots of different accents.
  • Cash vs. card habits can change; ask locals and your program what’s currently easiest for paying day-to-day.
