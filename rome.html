Budget snapshot for Rome

Rome can be surprisingly affordable for coffee and pizza slices, but rent near the center or close to major universities adds up. Many students report spending around €1,300 per month including housing, food, transit and going out a few times a week.

Example monthly budget:

  • Rent (shared apartment): ~€700
  • Groceries & espresso: ~€260
  • Public transport pass: ~€50
  • Going out & gelato: ~€220
  • Misc / buffer: ~€70

Why study in Rome?

In Rome, your walk to class might pass ruins older than your entire home country, tiny espresso bars, and laundry lines strung between apartments. Daily life is a mix of buses, long lunches and golden-hour walks through streets you’ve seen in movies.

The pace can feel slower than other capitals, but the city is dense with history and small neighborhood rituals. This guide is here to help you balance romantic expectations with the realistic parts: budgets, commutes and where students actually live.

Neighborhoods to know

Programs place students all over the city, but these areas come up a lot for international students.

Trastevere

Cobblestones · Nightlife · Riverfront · $$$

Picture-perfect alleys, ivy-covered façades and lots of bars and trattorie. It’s incredibly atmospheric and walkable, but prices and noise levels can be high, especially on weekends.

San Lorenzo

Student-heavy · Graffiti · Cheaper eats · $$

Close to La Sapienza and other universities, with lots of budget-friendly food and bars. Lively at night and more affordable than the historic center, but a bit scruffier around the edges.

Prati

Residential · Near Vatican · Wide streets · $$

Elegant apartment blocks, calmer streets and easy access to the Vatican and the river. Good if you like quieter nights and don’t mind taking the metro or bus to nightlife areas.

Quick tips for students

  • Coffee is cheaper at the bar than sitting at a table, as Romans usually drink their espresso standing up.
  • Monthly passes for buses and metro can save money if your campus is far from your housing.
  • Dinner starts later than in some countries; 8–9pm is normal, and restaurants can be quiet earlier.
  • Many smaller shops close mid-day or on Sundays. Always check hours before making a long trip.
